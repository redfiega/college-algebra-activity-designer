## 1. Course Context

**Course:** College Algebra (undergraduate, 4-year university)  
**Students:** First or second year undergraduates. Some math anxiety is common.  
**Class length:** 50 minutes  
**Activity window:** ~30 minutes (introduction through completion)  
**Class size:** Typical university section (varies; assume 20–35 students)

## 2. Rational Functions — Content Scope

A rational function is a function of the form f(x) = P(x) / Q(x), where P and Q are
polynomials and Q(x) ≠ 0.

### What students are expected to do in this unit:

**Simplify a rational expression**
- Factor the numerator and denominator completely
- Cancel any common factors
- State any restrictions on the domain (values of x that make the denominator zero)

**Find holes (removable discontinuities)**
- A hole occurs at x = a when (x − a) is a factor of BOTH the numerator and denominator
- After canceling, substitute x = a into the simplified function to find the y-coordinate
- Notation: write as an ordered pair (a, f(a))

**Find vertical asymptotes**
- A vertical asymptote occurs at x = a when (x − a) is a factor of the denominator
  AFTER simplification (i.e., it was not canceled out)
- Notation: write as x = a (a vertical line equation, not a point)

**Find horizontal asymptotes**
- Compare the degree of the numerator (n) to the degree of the denominator (m):
  - If n < m: horizontal asymptote is y = 0
  - If n = m: horizontal asymptote is y = (leading coefficient of numerator) /
    (leading coefficient of denominator)
  - If n > m: no horizontal asymptote (do NOT say oblique — this class does not cover
    polynomial division)
- Notation: write as y = k (a horizontal line equation)

**Find x-intercepts (zeros)**
- Set the simplified numerator equal to zero and solve
- Check that these values do not also make the denominator zero (those would be holes,
  not intercepts)
- Notation: write as ordered pairs (a, 0)

**Find the y-intercept**
- Substitute x = 0 into the original (unsimplified) function
- If x = 0 makes the denominator zero, there is no y-intercept
- Notation: write as an ordered pair (0, b)

### What is OUT OF SCOPE:
- Computing oblique (slant) asymptotes — polynomial long division is not covered
- Graphing the function from the features
- Polynomial long division of any kind
- End behavior analysis beyond horizontal asymptotes

### Important Clarifications for Evaluation:
- Functions where the numerator degree exceeds the denominator degree ARE valid
  and desirable. Students should recognize there is no horizontal asymptote and
  may be introduced to the vocabulary "oblique asymptote" without computing it.
  Do NOT flag these functions as out of scope or inappropriate.
- Rational functions that simplify to linear functions are excellent edge cases
  and ARE valid for activities. Do NOT flag these as needing replacement.
  They help students understand the relationship between holes and simplification.

## 3. Prerequisite Knowledge Students Should Have

- Factoring polynomials: GCF, trinomials (a=1 and a≠1), difference of squares
- Evaluating functions by substitution
- Solving linear and quadratic equations
- Understanding what a function is
- Basic fraction arithmetic

---

## 4. Common Student Errors

- Forgetting to factor before canceling (canceling terms instead of factors)
- Confusing holes with vertical asymptotes (both involve zeros of the denominator)
- Listing x-values instead of equations for asymptotes (writing "3" instead of "x = 3")
- Listing x-values as points instead of equations for vertical asymptotes
- Forgetting to check for holes when finding x-intercepts
- Incorrectly applying horizontal asymptote rules (mixing up the three cases)

## 5. Pedagogy Principles for This Context

### Structural Collaboration
Activities must be designed so that collaboration is *required*, not just encouraged.
Mechanisms that work:
- **Jigsaw:** Each student or pair has different information that must be combined
- **Role assignment:** Different students are responsible for different parts of the task
- **Shared resource with split ownership:** One whiteboard, but each person owns one
  section that others cannot touch
- **Consensus requirement:** Groups must agree on a single answer and justify it
- **Organic discussion:** Brief phases (5 minutes or less) where students discuss
  openly without assigned roles are valid and desirable. Examples include a quick
  card sort discussion, a group agreement on an approach, or a short debate about
  an answer. Do NOT penalize activities for including organic discussion phases.

### Important Clarifications for Collaboration Evaluation:
- Not every phase of an activity requires strict role assignment
- A short organic discussion phase (≤8 minutes) at the start or between structured
  phases is acceptable and does not weaken the collaboration structure
- Evaluate the activity as a whole — if the majority of the activity has structural
  interdependence, a brief organic discussion phase should not lower the score
- Only penalize activities where the ENTIRE activity relies on voluntary discussion
  with no structural mechanism at all

### Stretch Questions
Students engage most deeply when given problems that:
- Are solvable with knowledge they already have
- Require them to think in a new way or apply knowledge in an unfamiliar context
- Feel like a puzzle, not just a procedure

### Physical Resources Available
- Large tabletop whiteboards (one per group)
- Individual dry-erase markers (one per student)
- Math manipulatives (type varies)
- Printer, scissors, tape, standard office supplies
- Standard classroom furniture (moveable desks/chairs)

### Activity Format Variety (Beyond Card Sorts)
Encourage activities that use:
- Gallery walks (groups rotate to other groups' work and give feedback)
- Whiteboard relays (each student adds one step, passes the marker)
- Error analysis (students are given worked problems with mistakes to find and fix)
- Matching with justification (connect items but require written reasoning)
- "Convince me" tasks (one student explains, partner must agree or push back)
- Spectrum/sorting activities (place items on a continuum, not just categories)
- Incomplete information tasks (each group member has a piece; all pieces needed)

## 6. Activity Structure Template

A well-formed activity for this context includes:

1. **Title** — clear and descriptive
2. **Learning objectives** — what students will practice or demonstrate
3. **Time estimate** — broken into phases (intro, group work, debrief)
4. **Materials** — specific list of what is needed
5. **Setup instructions** — what the instructor does before class
6. **Student instructions** — written for students, step by step
7. **Collaboration mechanism** — explicit description of how collaboration is enforced
8. **Stretch question** — one additional challenge for groups that finish early
9. **Facilitator notes** — tips for the instructor during the activity
10. **Debrief prompts** — questions to drive whole-class discussion afterward
